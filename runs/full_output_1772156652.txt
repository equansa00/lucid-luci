Okay, beyond alcoholism, several other conditions show evidence that specific incentive-based approaches can significantly improve outcomes, sometimes leading to remission or substantial functional recovery. However, it's crucial to understand that "cure" often implies complete abstinence or absence of symptoms, and incentives are usually part of a broader treatment plan (therapy, medication, etc.). Here are a few examples:

1.  **Nicotine Use Disorder (Smoking Cessation):** This is one of the most well-documented examples. Financial incentives (e.g., cash rewards for negative urine tests, tax rebates) and contingency management (e.g., giving points for abstinence that can be exchanged for rewards) have been shown in numerous studies to significantly increase smoking cessation rates compared to standard advice alone. These methods leverage the brain's reward system by providing immediate positive reinforcement when the target behavior (abstinence) occurs, counteracting the immediate reward from nicotine.

2.  **Opioid Use Disorder (OUD):** While complete "cure" via incentives alone is less common, strong incentives are used effectively in medication-assisted treatment (MAT). For instance, providing rewards for adherence to medication (like buprenorphine or naltrexone) or for negative drug tests can help maintain engagement in treatment and reduce illicit opioid use. The goal here is often harm reduction and achieving sustained recovery, not necessarily a complete pharmacological "cure."

3.  **Gambling Disorder:** Similar to alcoholism, behavioral conditioning plays a role. While cognitive-behavioral therapy is key, some programs incorporate incentives, though less formally. This might involve setting personal financial goals (e.g., saving money instead of gambling) or tracking periods of abstinence to reward self-control. Financial loss from gambling often creates a powerful *negative* incentive, but structured positive incentives for sobriety can be part of relapse prevention.

4.  **Compulsive Overeating/Weight Loss Maintenance:** Incentives can be powerful tools for both weight loss (e.g., rewards for meeting weight loss goals) and maintaining weight loss. Financial incentives, points systems, or even non-financial rewards (like extra privileges) have shown effectiveness in clinical trials for both short-term weight loss and long-term maintenance, which can be seen as achieving a "cured" state (maintained healthy weight) for some individuals.

5.  **Trichotillomania (TTM) / Excoriation Disorder (ED):** These are body-focused repetitive behaviors (BFRBs) involving hair pulling or skin picking. While not always curable, cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) with habit reversal training is standard. Some research explores the use of incentives (like points systems rewarding time without the compulsion) as part of the behavioral modification component.

**Important Considerations:**

*   **Mechanism:** Incentives often work by leveraging operant conditioning principles – associating the absence of the unwanted behavior (e.g., not smoking, not relapsing) with a positive consequence.
*   **Context:** Incentive programs are most effective when integrated with other therapies (like CBT, medication, or support groups) that address the underlying psychological drivers of the behavior.
*   **Variability:** Effectiveness varies greatly depending on the individual, the specific disease, the type and magnitude of the incentive, and the method of delivery.

So, yes, beyond alcoholism, conditions like Nicotine Use Disorder, Opioid Use Disorder, Gambling Disorder, and even some eating behaviors or BFRBs show strong evidence that targeted incentives can play a significant role in achieving remission or maintaining recovery.
